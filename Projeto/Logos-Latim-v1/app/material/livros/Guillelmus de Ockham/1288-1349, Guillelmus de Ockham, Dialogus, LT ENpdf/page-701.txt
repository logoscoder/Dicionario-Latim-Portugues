Master The reply to you is that that
statute of Pelagius was both against the
gospel and against the freedom or right of
those subdeacons and their wives,
because he wanted to force the
subdeacons to be chaste, notwithstanding
the gospel and notwithstanding the
freedom or right of both the subdeacons
and their wives. For it was in the power
of the subdeacons, with the consent of
their wives, to be chaste and this was not
against the gospel. But Pope Pelagius
could not take that power away from the
subdeacons and their wives, as Gregory
attests. He was not able, therefore, to do
everything that was not against divine or
human law. And from this opinion of
Gregory we conclude that the pope can do
nothing against the freedom and right of
any christian, even in spiritual matters,
except by reason of fault or for some
clear cause, since no one can deprive
[another] of his right and his freedom
without some fault or for some reason.
And except by reason of fault or for some
cause, therefore, he can not enjoin on
anyone, except at his own bidding, fasting
to which he is not bound or alms to which
he is not bound or anything else similar.
