Master The reply is that the whole rest of
the world could not transfer the empire
from the Romans despite their opposition
without some fault of theirs or some clear
reason, because they should not be
deprived of their right without some fault
or reason. Nevertheless, if there were
some fault of the Romans or some reason,
the rest of the world could transfer the
empire from them, because, as we read in
dist. 93, c. Legimus [c.24, col.327], "The
world is greater than the city." This is not
only true of the world as it includes the
city, in that the whole is greater than a
part of it, but it also represents the truth
about the world as distinguished from the
city. And so the power of transferring for
a reason or because of a fault of the
Romans is in the power of the rest of the
world. However, the power of
transferring the empire secondarily is,
according to one opinion, in the power of
the Romans. For because anyone can cede
a right of his and grant it to another, the
Romans can cede the right that they have
over the empire and transfer that same
