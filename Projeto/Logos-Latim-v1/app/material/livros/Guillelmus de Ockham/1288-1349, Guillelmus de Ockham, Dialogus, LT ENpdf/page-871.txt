whom are known not to lead a spiritual and
virtuous life but a carnal and vicious one, so
that, by comparison of their life, laymen
have the power to judge them, even bishops
who are the greatest among the clerics.
Jerome attest to this when he says in 8, q. 1,
c. Vereor [c.22, col.597], "Very many
people judge bishops who withdraw from
their position in the church and do not
engage in those [activities] which befit a
bishop." The emperor has power over many
men called spiritual in the first sense
because many laymen subject to him should
be considered spiritual, taking the word in
that sense, since they are living according to
the spirit not according to the flesh. There
are opposed opinions about men called
spiritual in the second sense, some saying
that the emperor has no power over them
and others asserting the opposite.
